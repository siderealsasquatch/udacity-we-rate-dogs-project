Insights from WeRateDogs Data¶

The data wrangling process that we performed on the archived WeRateDogs data is simply preparation for the meat of the overall data analytics process: obtaining insights from our data. With the cleaned data on hand, we're ready to begin deriving insights. Before we can do this, we will need to import the necessary libraries as well as our data.

Tweet insights¶

Most common dog breeds¶

One of the first things we're interested in knowing is the most common dog breeds that appear in the tweets made by WeRateDogs. Unfortunately, this data is only available in the form of photos attached to the tweets in the archive. It would be a very tedious affair to download all of the pictures in each tweet and look through them manually. Thankfully we've also been provided with the image prediction results of a machine learning algorithm trained on a sample of the archived data. These results include the three predictions made by the algorithm, the level of confidence in each prediction expressed as a probability, and whether the prediction was correct. Since we weren't given information about the machine algorithm used to make these predictions, we're making the following assumptions about the results:

  • The predictions made by the algorithm are ordered. This means that the first prediction is the algorithm's "best guess" while the third prediction is the algorithm's worst.
  • There may be multiple correct predictions for a given tweet ID. In these cases we simply take the first correct prediction.
In [3]:
# Get all successful predictions, remove duplicates, and merge with twitter_archive
# Split this cell later to show that the number of dog breeds extracted from the
# image_predictions dataframe is not the same as the number of archived tweets.
dog_breeds = image_predictions.loc[image_predictions.prediction_success == True,
                                   ['tweet_id', 'prediction']]
dog_breeds.drop_duplicates('tweet_id', inplace=True)
twitter_archive = pd.merge(twitter_archive, dog_breeds, on='tweet_id', how='left')
twitter_archive.rename(columns={'prediction': 'dog_breed'}, inplace=True)

# Total number of dog breeds
twitter_archive.dog_breed.notnull().sum()
Out[3]:
1751

Notice that we were only able to extract dog breeds for 1751 tweets in our sample of 2355 tweets using the image prediction data which is about 74% of our sample. Assuming that the training set for the algorithm was a random sample of the archived tweets and that the archived tweets are themselves representative of the tweets made by WeRateDogs, the tweets for which we were able to extract dog breeds should be sufficient for any insights into the dog breeds represented in our sample to be meaningful.

Here we can see that, based on our sample of tweets, the dog breed that appears most often in WeRateDogs tweets even compared to the rest of the most common dog breeds is the Golden Retriever. In fact, it seems that the Golden Retriever is far and away the most common dog breed. Given the WeRateDogs account's huge following, this could be an indication of the overall popularity of not only the breeds shown in the chart but the other breeds in the sample as well. This is, of course, assuming that the people who follow WeRateDogs is representative of dog owners as a whole.

Most common dog type¶

One of the more charming and unique aspects of WeRateDogs is the way that dogs are referred to in the tweets. Dogs are called 'pupper', 'puppo', or 'doggo' based on how old the dog is with 'pupper' being used for puppieds, 'doggo' for adult dogs, and 'puppo' for dogs in a stage of development between puppy and adult. There is also the word 'floofer' used to describe dogs of any age with a particularly fluffy coat. We'd like to know which of these words appears the most in the archived tweets.

In [5]:
# Plot counts of each dog type
plot_category_order = ['pupper', 'doggo', 'puppo', 'floofer']
plt.figure(dpi=100);
ax = sns.countplot(x='dog_type', data=twitter_archive, order=plot_category_order);
ax.set_title('Counts of each Dog Type');
ax.set_ylabel('Count');
ax.set_xlabel('Dog Type');

It would appear that the most commonly used term is 'pupper' indicating that the majority of the dogs shown in WeRateDogs are puppies. However, we should remember that the total number of tweets in our sample is 2355 and that it's not required for the tweets to contain any of these words. If we include the tweets that don't contain these words we get a more accurate frequency count.

In [6]:
# Plot counts of dog types and include missing values
dog_type_counts_nulls = (twitter_archive.dog_type.value_counts(dropna=False)

Here we see that the overwhelming majority of the tweets do not refer to the dogs using any of these words. Thus, we actually don't have a good idea of the general age of the dogs shown in the tweets of WeRateDogs.

WeRateDogs account insights¶

Total tweets per month¶

The timestamps contained in the archived tweet data give us access to a new dimension of analysis as we are now able to view trends over periods of time. Let's use the provided timestamps to gain some insight into how often WeRateDogs posts tweets. Keep in mind that the account was started in November 15, 2015.

In [8]:
# Prepare tweet_archive for plotting
twitter_archive['timestamp'] = pd.to_datetime(twitter_archive['timestamp'])
twitter_archive['year_month_date'] = twitter_archive['timestamp'].dt.year.astype(str) + '-' + \
                                     twitter_archive['timestamp'].dt.month.astype(str).str.pad(2, fillchar='0')
twitter_archive['is_tweet'] = np.where(twitter_archive.tweet_id.notnull(), 1, 0)

# Get required data from twitter_archive
twitter_archive_monthly_tweets = twitter_archive.groupby('year_month_date').is_tweet.sum().reset_index()

# Plot the data
plt.figure(dpi=100);
plt.xticks(rotation=45);
ax = sns.lineplot(x='year_month_date', y='is_tweet', data=twitter_archive_monthly_tweets);
ax.set_title('Total Tweets made by WeRateDogs per Month');
ax.set_xlabel('Date');
ax.set_ylabel('Number of Tweets');

From the plot, we see that the total tweets per month peaked with 388 tweets in December of 2015 and gradually decreased throughout 2016 and the majority of 2017. Although there were periods where the number of tweets have increased, there is a general downward trend. This seems to indicate that, when the account was first started, the people behind the account had more time to tweet, time that diminished over the almost two years that the account has been active at the time this data was collected. A more recent archive of WeRateDog tweets which includes dates up to the current day may show a new trend.

Summary¶

Tweet object data taken from Twitter is quite rich and allows for a great many insights to be gained. Even the basic data from a user's Twitter archive is enough to derive a plethora of insights such as the ones described in this report. For example, we found that the most common dog breed tweeted by WeRateDogs is the Golden Retriever. We also found that the number of tweets made by WeRateDogs per month has been decreasing since the accounts creation and that, while WeRateDogs tends not to reply to tweets the account prefers to reply to more well known Twitter users. While there are still many insights that can be obtained from the data that we didn't explore, the insights that we did gain are all immediately useful and demonstrate the importance of clean data.
